How to write LinkedIn posts that don't get truncated

The "see more" fold is the most important real estate on LinkedIn. Here's the math, the psychology, and the templates.

The 140/210 character fold

On mobile, LinkedIn truncates your post at approximately 140 characters (3 lines of body text). On desktop, that fold extends to roughly 210 characters. If your hook — the opening line that earns the click — sits below this fold, your post dies in the feed.

Always assume the mobile viewer first. That's where ~80% of impressions happen. If your first 140 characters don't earn the tap on "see more," nothing else matters.

Why the hook decides everything

LinkedIn's algorithm measures dwell time — the seconds a viewer spends on your post before scrolling past. A weak hook triggers an instant scroll. The algorithm reads that as low-quality content and stops distributing it.

A strong hook — curiosity, controversy, specificity, or story — pulls the reader past the fold. Dwell time spikes. The algorithm pushes your post to the next 1,000 viewers. Compounding distribution.

The Unicode bold trick

LinkedIn's editor strips Markdown, HTML, and rich text. You cannot bold a word the normal way. But you can use Unicode Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols — the same characters used in math textbooks since the 1990s.

Type a normal "A" and it renders as "A." Type the Unicode "𝗔" and it renders as "𝗔" — bold, sans-serif, unstripped by LinkedIn. The same works for italics (𝘈), script (𝒜), and monospace (𝙰).

Select any word in the editor above and click B to convert. The visual weight alone increases scan-ability by ~40% in feeds.

The 3,000 character ceiling

LinkedIn caps posts at 3,000 characters. Posts in the 1,200–1,800 character range consistently outperform both ultra-short posts (under 400) and maxed-out posts (2,800+). The sweet spot gives enough room for a hook, three beats, and a CTA without forcing a "see more" on desktop viewers.

The character counter in the editor warns you the moment you cross the 1,200 and 1,800 marks.

The line break trick most creators miss

Single-line paragraphs are death on mobile. They look lazy, they don't breathe, and they collapse into the fold. Replace every period-paragraph with a single line break — one sentence per visual line.

This single formatting change is responsible for more engagement lift than any "growth hack" ever sold by a SaaS tool. Try it on your next post and watch the dwell time.

FAQ

Common questions

What is the LinkedIn post character limit in 2026?

LinkedIn posts have a 3,000 character hard limit. But the more important number is the feed truncation: ~140 characters on mobile (3 lines), ~210 characters on desktop, before the "see more" button appears. Your hook must fit in this fold to drive engagement.

How do you bold text on LinkedIn?

LinkedIn's native editor doesn't support bold. The trick is Unicode Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols — type your text normally, select the words you want bolded, and our tool converts each character to its Unicode equivalent (e.g., "A" → "𝗔"). LinkedIn doesn't strip these because they're treated as normal text characters.

Is this LinkedIn previewer really free and private?

Yes. No account, no email, no payment. The entire tool is a single HTML page with vanilla JavaScript — when you load it, your browser downloads the static files and runs everything locally. Your draft posts never reach a server.

Does the preview match LinkedIn exactly?

We replicate LinkedIn's feed DOM structure, body typography (Source Serif 4, matching LinkedIn's published font stack), spacing tokens, avatar placeholders, and the 140/210 character truncation points across mobile (360px) and desktop (552px) widths. Edge cases like emoji rendering match your system defaults.

Can I use the formatted text directly in LinkedIn?

Yes. Click "Copy formatted" and paste straight into LinkedIn's composer. Unicode characters render identically on LinkedIn, mobile, desktop, and in notifications.

Why not just use ChatGPT to format my post?

You can — but LLMs hallucinate, they don't show you the actual fold, and they require you to paste your draft into a third-party server. For deterministic, fast, private formatting, a purpose-built tool wins every time.
